Their analysis reveals a fundamental dichotomy in the phase diagram: for the Maxwell case, all nonzero relevant branches are acausal, while causal branches are free of caustics. To handle the unstable vacuum, the authors use the Keldysh-Schwinger-Fradkin nonequilibrium technique, deriving the mean photon number density up to second order in the fine-structure constant. Key results include the identification of vertex, tadpole, interference, loop, and induced-current contributions at order α², as well as the computation of the mean electromagnetic field up to order e³. The work extends standard in-out QED to unstable vacua, providing a general framework applicable to arbitrary spacetime-dependent backgrounds.|
|**2026-06-12**|**Exceptional Points as Manifestations of Analyticity Breakdown in the 't Hooft Model**|Kejun Liu|[[arxiv:2606.10141](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.10141)]|The paper studies the breakdown of analyticity in the meson two-point function of the 't Hooft model, an exactly solvable 1+1D QCD theory, by introducing a PT-symmetric deformation. The method involves tracking how the resolvent's poles coalesce at an exceptional point (EP), which is identified analytically using a Jacobi continued fraction. The key result is that the EP occurs at a coupling strength \(\gamma_c \approx 7.966g^2N_c\), with the square-root exponent \(\nu = 1/2\) confirmed by finite-size scaling up to \(N=1999\), and the threshold is proportional to the confinement scale. This provides the first analytically controlled example of exceptional-point-induced analyticity breakdown in a confining gauge theory, with clear time-domain signatures observable in non-Hermitian analogues.|

The study finds that classical spins exhibit a diverse array of noncollinear magnetic orders, while quantum fluctuations suppress many of these competing orders, resulting in fewer dominant incommensurate phases. Additionally, highly frustrated regions are identified where spiral spin liquids and new magnetically ordered states can be stabilized by other interactions. These results offer a comprehensive guide for interpreting experiments on Kitaev candidate materials and for designing new materials to realize the Kitaev spin liquid.|

They introduce a stochastic reward based on one-shot energy measurements to enable training in a way compatible with real experiments. The method successfully prepares ground states of the Bose–Hubbard model and generates GHZ states, demonstrating a scalable and hardware-friendly approach to quantum state preparation.|

They find that the Hall response is highly sensitive to particle-hole asymmetry and Fermi surface topology, revealing a crossover from semi-classical to quantum-coherent transport that is obscured in the longitudinal channel. The results demonstrate that the Hall effect provides unique microscopic insights into quantum coherence not captured by resistivity alone.|
